01 ReBox App Design

This all began as a project for a 24 hour hackathon, however, 24 hours wasn’t enough for me. I really wanted to fully flush out the project and go full UX designer on the idea. Here’s where I ended up.

Role

UX/UI Design, Prototyping, Usertesting, Competitive Analysis

Duration

6 weeks (February-March 2019)
Part-Time

How it started

Given the task of tackling sustaible health my team and I came up with the idea of taking on the challenge of take out container waste. After the 24 hour period we had a cute XD prototype that had some features but I wasn’t satisfied. I wanted to go full UX on this sh** so this is where I landed.

Why I continued

As much as we like to think we are sustainable gurus here in Vancouver I am sad to say, we most certainly are not.

"Disposable cups and take-out containers make up roughly half of the garbage that's tossed in Vancouver's public waste bins"

-Monica Kosmak

How might we get rid of disposable containers forever...

ReBox: The reusable take out container service.

Convenient, easy and sustainable.

Goals

Make it the New Norm

Replace preconceived notions about how takeout is practiced. No longer associated with disposable.

Reduce Waste In VanCity

At the moment HALF of the trash in our garbage cans is disposable containers. ReBox seeks to take actiona against this.

Incentivise Continued Use

It is one thing to get people to try your product, let alone use it on a regular basis! ReBox seeks to be so seemless that users will come back again and again.

Design Process

Organizing a hierarchy of information was critical for ReBox. Continued research and iteration was crucial to understanding users and how to best tailor ReBox to their needs and motivations.

Step 1: Survey's and Interviews

In order to get to know my users better I created a survey with 20 respondents and conducted interviews with 5 individuals. I find this to be a good jumping off point for user research as it gives you some idea towards general tendencies and opinions. It allowed me to focus my efforts on delving into users habits on a much narrower scope. The general things that I was trying to identify was who ReBox’s users were; their habits, age, motivations, core needs and pain points. Ultimately this phase helped me figure out what my early adopter target market looked like.

Key Findings

  • 70% of individuals order take out AT LEAST once a month
  • 55% of individuals order take out AT LEAST once a week
  • Individuals order take out for themselves usually during lunch and in a group for dinner
  • 75% of Individuals are more likely to reuse a service if there is some sort of incentive system involved
  • Convenience was more important to respondents than being responsible
  • The main motivators in peoples lives as University students was convenience, sustainability, social, and practicality
  • The majority of people are concerned about climate change but find it difficult to take action in their everyday lives.
  • 75% of Individuals order take out from the same restaurants
  • The main motivators in peoples lives as young professionals was convenience, sustainability, efficiency, and practicality

Defining Users Main Goals

  • Easy. Short lunch breaks.

  • Convenient.
  • Practical. Don’t want to do the dishes.
  • Fast. Pre order?--> maybe partnership with food ordering services

Information Architecture

The information architecture map helped me to visualize what top level elements would be and how they would relate to the other features. ReBox was relatively straight forward as their isn’t a huge amount of overlap between information.

Feature Flows

Feature flows allow me to explore the different paths and branches that users can take while completing different tasks. An insight gained from this exercise was that users have to enable their camera in order to use the app. This step can be completed during the on-boarding and sign up.

User Flows

The purpose of the user flows are to identify the main paths the user is going to take through the app. By focusing on the main tasks that users will need to complete I was able to figure out how to tailor specific services to these use cases. An example would be with Dan the young professional, having a drop box around or even in his building would be convenient and good user experience.

User Stories

The user case story delves into a potential users interaction with the app from discovery to continued use. It is a good exercise to identify how users will come across the app, how it fits into their daily life and what might incentivize them to continue using the app. From this exercise a major take away was that users will want a simple option to split the bill if they are ordering in a group.

Competitive Analysis

In order to assess the level of feasibility for this project I looked at some other similar business models and apps in order to see how other’s were already tackling similar problems.

Feasibility

During my research phase it became extremely evident that industry infrastructure was going to be a big concern. For example having the pickup vehicles to pick up the empty containers from drop cites, a commercial kitchen to clean all of the containers, a B2B app to deal with affiliated vendors and having enough workers to make it all run smoothly.

Paper Prototyping

Paper Prototyping is a great exercise as it allows you to quickly create a physical product that you can get users to interact with. You are able to mitigate issues before you spend hours building out high fidelity mocks. Some of the big insights I gained from my paper prototyping phase was that users were not a fan of the toggle option on the map and the rewards page as it was confusing. As well the concern was raised regarding inputting credit card information.

Wireframes

Wire-framing with my actual copy was incredibly important as it allowed me to iron out any odd titles that were to long and other usability issues before I committed to final designs. I try my best to stay away from UI kits as I feel they can influence the final design too much, not to say I don’t take full advantage of them for different attributes.

User Testing

User Testing takes place after each stage of paper prototyping and wire framing. It is an incredibly important part of my process as I feel that it allows me to tailor my design to users specific needs. Some key insights that I got from my user testing was that I should use a QR Code instead of a barcode and that if I could gamify the reward it would enhance the user experience.
